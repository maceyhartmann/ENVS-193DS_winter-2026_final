Problem 1. Research writing

a. Transparent statistical methods

In part 1, they used a simple linear regression, because they are testing whether a single continuous predictor variable (precipitation) predicts a continuous response variable (flooded wetland area). In part 2, they used a Kruskal-Wallis test, because they are comparing the median of a continuous response variable (flooded wetland area) across more than two groups of a single categorical predictor variable (water year classification: wet, above normal, below normal, dry, critical drought).

b. Figure needed

The coworker should make a jitter plot with an overlaid box plot. The x-axis should display water year classification as a categorical variable with five groups (wet, above normal, below normal, dry, critical drought), and the y-axis should display flooded wetland area in m². The boxplot component should show the median as a horizontal line within the box, with the box itself representing the interquartile range and whiskers extending to the full range of the data as these elements directly correspond to the Kruskal-Wallis test’s comparison of medians across groups. The jittered points overlaid on each box should show the underlying raw data for each water year classification, giving the reader a sense of spread and sample size within each group. Using different colors for each water year category would further help a non-statistical audience visually distinguish between the five groups.

b. Understanding the data

The observation from 2000-09-22 at transect 6, quad 40 has a frond count value of -99999, which is a value used to indicate missing data (either because data were not collected for that species or because a datasheet was lost, as noted in the Methods and protocols section of the data page). This value is filtered out because -99999 does not represent a real frond count and would skew any calculations of mean fronds if retained.

d. Interpretation

The Isla Vista site shows the most variable mean kelp fronds per year, as evidenced by the dramatic peaks and troughs in its line. The Isla Vista site spikes to nearly 40 fronds around 2008 and again to ~38 fronds around 2022, then drops to near or below 5 fronds in several intervening years, producing the largest overall range and most erratic trajectory of the three sites.

The Carpinteria site shows the least variable mean kelp fronds per year, as its line remains relatively flat and close to the bottom of the plot for the entire time series, generally fluctuating between approximately 3 and 13 fronds per year with no dramatic spikes, indicating a much smaller and more consistent range compared to the other two sites.

Problem 3. Data analysis

a. Response variable

In this data set, a 1 indicates that a nest site was actively used by a Mountain Plover for nesting. A 0 indicates an unused site, specifically a randomly selected point within the same colony where no plovers were detected and no nests were observed during surveys in that year.

b. Purpose of study

Visual obstruction (vo_nest) is a continuous variable measuring the height at which vegetation is sufficiently dense to obscure a 2.5-cm wide pole when viewed from a 1-m height, incorporating both vegetation height and density. As visual obstruction increases, the probability of Mountain Plover nest site use would decrease because Mountain Plovers prefer sparse, short vegetation that allows them to detect approaching ground predators while still providing some concealment for a sitting bird. This information was found in paragraph 4 of the discussion section.

Distance to prairie dog colony edge (edgedist) is a continuous variable measuring how far a given point is from the nearest edge of the prairie dog colony. As distance to colony edge increases, the probability of Mountain Plover nest site use would decrease because plovers tend to nest closer to colony edges where complementary resources such as taller vegetation for thermoregulation and predator cover are more accessible for adults traveling with broods. This information was found in paragraph 2 of the discussion section.
